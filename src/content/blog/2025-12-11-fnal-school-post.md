This year taught me a lot, but the most valuable takeaways were not single facts or isolated skills. They were patterns. Things I can reuse in other classes, projects, and work settings. If I had to boil it down, these are the three lessons I’m carrying forward.

## 1) Good work is usually the result of iteration, not inspiration

Most of the strongest outcomes this year came from building something, breaking it, fixing it, and repeating that loop until it actually held up. Whether it was writing, code, or a project deliverable, the first version was rarely the best version.

The biggest mindset shift was treating drafts as a normal part of the process instead of proof that I did something “wrong.” When I started expecting revision, I got faster at improving, less stuck when something failed, and more confident about shipping work that was actually polished.

## 2) Communication is part of the technical skill, not an extra

I used to think of explanations, documentation, and structure as “presentation.” This year made it clear that they are part of the actual engineering.

If you cannot clearly state what you built, why it works, where it fails, and how someone else can use it, the project is not done. Clarity is not fluff. It is what makes work transferable, testable, and credible. That applies to writeups, comments, READMEs, diagrams, and even how you talk through a solution with teammates.

## 3) Real systems break in real ways (and you learn the most when you debug honestly)

A lot of learning happened when things went sideways: unexpected outputs, edge cases, confusing errors, or systems that worked in one environment and failed in another.

The practical lesson is that “it runs” is not the same thing as “it is correct.” The deeper lesson is that debugging is not just hunting errors. It is building a habit of careful reasoning: checking assumptions, isolating variables, testing the smallest possible case, and documenting what you tried so you do not repeat the same dead ends.

That process trained me to be more patient and more systematic, which ends up saving time.

## A suggestion for future classes

If I could add one thing to future versions of these classes, it would be more structured practice around **real-world workflow**: small checkpoints, clear rubrics for what “done” means, and short feedback cycles that encourage iteration.

Even something simple, like requiring a brief README, a short reflection on tradeoffs, or a “known issues” section for projects, would push learning in a direction that matches how work happens outside the classroom. It also helps students get comfortable admitting what is unfinished, what is uncertain, and what they would improve next.

## Closing thought

Overall, I’m leaving this year with more confidence, not because everything was easy, but because I now trust my process. I know how to iterate, explain, and debug my way forward, and that’s the kind of learning that sticks.
